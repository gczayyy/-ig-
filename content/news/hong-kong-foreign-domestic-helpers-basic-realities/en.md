## I. Basic Situation

### Scale and Composition

Hong Kong is one of the largest importers of foreign domestic helpers in Asia. According to data from the Hong Kong Immigration Department and the Census and Statistics Department, as of **end-2024**, Hong Kong had approximately **367,971 foreign domestic helpers** (FDHs), accounting for about **9.6%** of the local workforce. This figure represents a decline from the 2019 peak (approximately 399,000), but has been steadily recovering from the pandemic low (approximately 340,000 in 2021).

Source country distribution (end-2024):
- **Philippines**: approximately 55.16% (~203,000)
- **Indonesia**: approximately 42.28% (~156,000)
- The remaining ~2.56% come from Thailand, Sri Lanka, Bangladesh, India, Nepal, and others

Gender composition: nearly **90% are women**. According to the MFMW/APMM 2025 survey, respondents had lived/worked in Hong Kong for an average of approximately **77 months** (~6.4 years).

### Nature of Work

The work performed by FDHs encompasses household cleaning and cooking, childcare, elderly care (Hong Kong's aging population continues to drive up demand), and other domestic tasks. According to government statistics, in 2023, 11% of elderly persons living alone employed an FDH, and the labour force participation rate of married women of childbearing age with FDH assistance reached **82.3%** (compared to only 45.6% for those without).

The official designation is "domestic helper," not "worker." This terminology carries substantive legal implications ? FDHs are exempted under Schedule 2 of the *Employment Ordinance* (Cap. 57) from multiple core labour protections applicable to local workers, including working hours regulation, overtime pay, and rest day protections, and are excluded from the scope of the statutory minimum wage under the *Minimum Wage Ordinance* (Cap. 608) (see Section 2.1).

### Social and Economic Contribution

Through their care labour, FDHs sustain the daily lives of hundreds of thousands of Hong Kong households and release local labour (especially women) into the formal employment market. At the same time, FDHs contribute over **HK$374.5 million per month** to the Hong Kong economy through local shopping and service consumption (MFMW/APMM 2025 estimate).

## II. Policy and Institutional Framework

### 2.1 The Dual-Wage System: Separation of MAW from the Statutory Minimum Wage

FDHs in Hong Kong are not covered by the **Statutory Minimum Wage (SMW)** established under the *Minimum Wage Ordinance* (Cap. 608). Instead, they are subject only to the government's separately set **Minimum Allowable Wage (MAW)** ? a wage standard enforced through the Standard Employment Contract (ID 407) and immigration policy, rather than through legislative procedure.

| Wage Standard | Amount | Applicable To | Legal Basis |
|---------------|--------|---------------|-------------|
| SMW (from 1 May 2025) | HK$42.1/hour | Local and non-exempt workers | *Minimum Wage Ordinance* (Cap. 608) |
| MAW (from 30 Sep 2025) | HK$5,100/month | FDHs | Immigration Dept. Standard Employment Contract (ID 407) |
| Food allowance (if employer does not provide free meals) | HK$1,236/month | FDHs | Ibid. |

Note: The previous MAW was HK$4,990/month (applicable from September 2024 to 29 September 2025).

The government maintains the dual-wage system on the grounds of "live-in arrangements," "board and lodging provided," and "difficulty in calculating working hours." FDH groups and civil society organisations have calculated a **living wage** (sufficient to cover basic living expenses in Hong Kong plus remittances to sustain family and children's education reproduction) of approximately **HK$6,230?6,320/month**, leaving a gap of roughly HK$1,130?1,220 with the MAW.

According to the 2025 questionnaire survey of 1,393 FDHs conducted by Mission for Migrant Workers (MFMW) and Asia Pacific Mission for Migrants (APMM):
- 58% of respondents received the MAW (then HK$4,990) or below
- 14.14% of respondents received wages below HK$4,870
- 88.08% of respondents did not receive the government-stipulated food allowance (then HK$1,236/month)

### 2.2 The Visa?Employer Bondage System

FDHs enter Hong Kong on work visas. Under section 11 of the *Immigration Ordinance* (Cap. 115), the Director of Immigration may impose **conditions of stay** on persons permitted to remain in Hong Kong. The conditions of stay for FDHs include: **visa tied to a single employer** ? the sole basis for lawful residence is "employment with a designated employer performing domestic duties at a designated address." Without the approval of the Director of Immigration, FDHs may not change employers or engage in any paid work outside the contract.

Core legal consequences:
- Loss of designated employer ? conditions of stay no longer satisfied ? visa becomes invalid
- Visa becomes invalid ? must depart Hong Kong within 14 days (see Section 2.3)
- During the processing of labour disputes, the Immigration Department **generally does not approve new work visas** (frontline actors estimate that applications for employer change are rejected in over 90% of cases)
- Breach of conditions of stay (e.g., working for persons other than the contract employer) is a criminal offence, punishable by a maximum fine of HK$50,000 and imprisonment for 2 years; employing a person not lawfully employable is punishable by a maximum fine of HK$500,000 and imprisonment for 10 years (Cap. 115, ss. 41, 38AA)

Furthermore, under section 2(4)(a)(vi) of the *Immigration Ordinance*, FDHs are **not regarded as "ordinarily resident"** during their stay in Hong Kong ? this means that regardless of how many years an FDH continuously resides in Hong Kong, she is ineligible to apply for permanent residency status. This provision was unanimously upheld as constitutional by the Court of Final Appeal in *Vallejos v. Commissioner of Registration* (2013).

### 2.3 The "Two-Week Rule" ? The Stay Condition Termination Mechanism

In April 1987, the Hong Kong government administratively implemented the "New Conditions of Stay" (commonly known as the "Two-Week Rule"), incorporating them as a fixed condition of stay into the Standard Employment Contract for FDHs. Under this condition, upon premature termination of the employment contract, an FDH may remain in Hong Kong for **14 days** (or until the expiry of the permitted stay period, whichever is earlier). Within these 14 days, the FDH must handle all of the following:
- File a labour complaint with the Labour Department
- Seek legal assistance
- Organise documentary evidence
- Find temporary accommodation without income
- Process departure or apply for a change of employer under special circumstances

Failure to complete the complaint/extension application within 14 days = loss of the right to seek remedies in Hong Kong = compulsory departure = disappearance from institutional statistics.

In practice, completing all of the above steps within 14 days ? without professional legal support ? is virtually impossible for an ordinary FDH. **Information asymmetry and time pressure** together constitute a substantive exclusion from the channels of rights protection.

Exceptions: Where the employer has died, emigrated/been posted overseas, faces financial difficulties, or where there is evidence of abuse/exploitation, or where the FDH needs to attend Labour Tribunal hearings or assist in criminal investigations, the Immigration Department may, at its discretion, grant an extension of stay as a visitor. No work is permitted during the extension period.

### 2.4 Labour Tribunal Processing Timeline

The Hong Kong Labour Tribunal's initial handling of FDH labour disputes is statistically relatively efficient ? according to the Judiciary's 2024 Annual Report, the average time from filing to the first mention hearing is approximately **23 days** (2024 data; target: 30 days), and from appointment booking to filing is approximately 36 days. However, this only covers the initial hearing stage. For FDH cases involving complex factual disputes, multiple adjournments, employer non-attendance requiring re-scheduling, and post-judgment enforcement, frontline actors, based on practical experience, point out that the full processing cycle can extend to **11 months or even longer**. During this period:
- The case remains pending; the Immigration Department generally refuses to issue a new work visa
- The FDH cannot work lawfully ? no income ? cannot send remittances home ? family reproduction is interrupted
- Must still pay for food and accommodation in Hong Kong
- Income loss over 11 months is approximately HK$56,000 (calculated at the MAW of HK$5,100/month)

Labour Tribunal procedures tend to promote **conciliation** (generally conducted at the first mention stage). Under conditions of extreme power asymmetry between worker and employer ? where the worker cannot afford the prolonged wait following failed conciliation ? whether conciliation agreements genuinely reflect workers' rights faces structural??.

### 2.5 Mandatory Live-in Requirement and Working Hours Regulation Exemption

Under the Immigration Department's Standard Employment Contract (ID 407), FDHs are required to **reside in the employer's home** (live-in requirement). At the legal level, Schedule 2 of the *Employment Ordinance* (Cap. 57) expressly exempts employees engaged in "domestic service" from multiple core labour protection provisions, including:

- **Working hours regulation**: no statutory maximum working hours
- **Overtime pay**: no legal provision for overtime compensation
- **Rest day protection**: statutory rest day provisions do not apply to domestic employees (in practice, FDHs' rest days are governed by contract, typically one day per week)

This means:
- The workplace and living space are one and the same ? "off duty" does not exist in a physical sense
- The FDH remains within the employer's sphere of control 24 hours a day
- Frontline actors, based on case experience, report that actual daily work of 10?12 hours is the norm, with 18 hours not uncommon
- Unable to calculate wages on an hourly basis ? unable to claim overtime pay
- The monthly salary system packages labour hours far exceeding those of local workers as a fixed-wage "board and lodging" arrangement

A rough comparison using the statutory minimum hourly wage of HK$42.1/hour effective from May 2025 as a benchmark: at 12 hours per day and 26 days per month, equivalent working hours under the SMW framework would yield a monthly wage of approximately HK$13,100 ? the current MAW (HK$5,100/month) represents only about 39% of this amount. It should be noted that due to the particularities of domestic labour (uneven work intensity, mixed labour content), this comparison is for reference only and does not constitute a legal standard.

### 2.6 Contract Termination Clauses: Formal Equality and Substantive Inequality

The Standard Employment Contract (ID 407) provides that either party may terminate the contract by giving one month's written notice, or by making one month's payment in lieu of notice. The employer must also notify the Immigration Department **within 7 days** of contract termination (Form ID 407E) and bear the cost of the FDH's **return airfare** to her place of origin, plus a daily travel and food allowance of approximately HK$100.

This clause formally grants both parties symmetrical termination rights, but in substance, the exit costs for each party are highly asymmetrical:

- The employer's cost of terminating the contract: one month's payment in lieu of notice + return airfare + long service payment (if applicable), typically totalling within HK$10,000
- The FDH's cost of terminating the contract (or being dismissed by the employer and unable to change employers): visa invalidation + departure within 14 days + loss of lawful income source in Hong Kong + interruption of family remittances + irrecoverable placement fees already paid

### 2.7 Loss of Medical Entitlements Upon Contract Termination

During the validity of the contract, FDHs are classified as "Eligible Persons" at public hospitals, enjoying the same charges as Hong Kong residents: HK$180 per Accident & Emergency attendance, and HK$120 per day for general ward hospitalisation. Employers are legally required to bear the full medical expenses of FDHs (Clause 9 of the Standard Employment Contract).

Once the contract is terminated, FDHs immediately lose "Eligible Person" status and are reclassified as "Non-eligible Persons." Under the Hospital Authority's revised charges effective January 2026, charges for Non-eligible Persons at public hospitals are as follows:

- Accident & Emergency: **HK$2,100** per attendance
- General ward hospitalisation: **HK$7,400** per day (was HK$5,100 before end-2025)
- Specialist outpatient: HK$850 per attendance + HK$90 per drug item

The daily hospitalisation charge of HK$7,400 far exceeds an FDH's monthly salary (MAW HK$5,100/month) ? in the event of contract termination, a single routine hospitalisation could exhaust more than one month's wages.

This institutional arrangement objectively provides employers with an economic incentive: when a worker shows early signs of illness (requiring regular check-ups, imaging examinations, etc.), rather than continuing employment and bearing the medical costs, it is cheaper to pay one month's payment in lieu of notice and dismiss her ? the economic cost of the latter is far lower than the former. Frontline actors have reported fatal cases resulting from this mechanism.

## III. Real-World Predicaments

### 3.1 Income and Expenditure Structure: A Locked-in Economic State

The following data comes from the 2025 MFMW and APMM questionnaire survey of 1,393 FDHs (at that time, the MAW was HK$4,990/month):

| Indicator | Data |
|-----------|------|
| Regular remittance rate | 96.84% |
| Average monthly remittance amount | ~HK$2,689 |
| Remittances as a proportion of monthly income | ~63.98% |
| Loan/placement fee as a proportion of monthly income | ~13.63% |
| Respondents with loan expenses | ~23.7% |
| Among those in Hong Kong less than one year: proportion still paying monthly placement fees | 57% |
| Respondents without regular savings | 73.80% |
| Respondents with monthly negative balance (expenditure > income) | 26% |
| Average monthly deficit | ~?9.15% of monthly income |

### 3.2 Placement Fees and Debt Bondage

Before coming to Hong Kong, FDHs typically must pay placement fees exceeding **HK$10,000** to recruitment agencies (covering recruitment, training, visa processing, medical examination, airfare, etc.). Calculated against the MAW of HK$5,100/month, this means that approximately **2?3 months' wages** are entirely devoted to repaying placement fees. During this period, FDHs are in an extremely weak position to resist any form of rights violation ? interruption of employment means the debt cannot be repaid and all upfront investment is sunk.

Although the 2025 survey shows a decline in loan and placement fee expenditure compared to 2018, among those in Hong Kong for less than one year, 57% are still paying monthly placement fees. The MFMW/APMM report explicitly notes that the problem of continuing to charge placement fees after the contract takes effect remains serious, constituting a risk factor for **debt bondage**.

### 3.3 Remittance Pressure and the "Uninterruptible" Reproduction Chain

Approximately 97% of FDHs regularly send remittances to their countries of origin, accounting for nearly two-thirds of their monthly income on average. These remittances are primarily used for:
- Basic living expenses of family members (food, housing, medical care)
- Children's education costs
- Repayment of existing debts in the country of origin

Typical structure: a single FDH in Hong Kong needs to sustain the basic livelihood of 3?4 family members in rural Philippines or Indonesia. This makes "interruption of remittances" not merely an economic issue ? it means the interruption of the family's basic survival security and children dropping out of school. The interruptibility of the FDH's employment relationship in Hong Kong, and the uninterruptibility of the family reproduction chain in her home country, constitute a structural contradiction.

### 3.4 Economic Constraints on the Capacity to Seek Remedies

When **73.80% have no regular savings and 26% have a monthly negative balance**, the following costs of seeking remedies are, in practical terms, unaffordable:
- Unable to work lawfully during the labour dispute processing period ? zero income
- Full processing cycle of 11 months (or longer) ? cumulative income loss of approximately HK$56,000
- No savings means that upon unemployment, basic food and accommodation capacity is lost ? even within the 14-day complaint window period

This forms a closed loop: **low wages ? high remittance pressure ? no savings ? remittances cannot be interrupted ? unemployment unaffordable ? unable to resist rights violations ? deepened dependency**. Workers are not "unwilling to seek remedies," but are financially and temporally unable to bear the costs of doing so.

### 3.5 Sexual Harassment and Gender-Based Violations

FDHs are overwhelmingly women, and the mandatory live-in condition places their bodies continuously within physical spaces directly controllable by the employer:

- **Sexual harassment by male employers**: Court testimony records cited by frontline actors show that employer harassment takes forms including, but not limited to, sexually suggestive verbal comments, non-consensual physical contact, and more serious misconduct
- **Jealousy and degradation by female employers**: Some female employers transform marital anxiety into oppressive behaviour toward FDHs, including, but not limited to, insulting degradation of personhood, unfounded accusations of theft, and more

The reason FDHs dare not immediately resist after suffering violations is not only fear of retaliation ? it is also economic constraint: placement fee debts remain unpaid, and interruption of remittances means the family's basic survival is threatened, forcing them into a "deliberation period" after being violated.

## IV. Structural Predicament

The policy issues and real-world predicaments described above do not exist in isolation. They constitute an **interlocking structure** ? in which the loosening of any single link is constrained by other links, and single-dimensional reforms struggle to produce fundamental change.

### 4.1 The Operational Logic of the Interlocking Mechanism

```
Stay conditions tied to employer ? cannot change employer ? must endure violations
       ?                                    ?
  14-day departure window  ? no lawful status     deepened dependency
       ?                    after dismissal            ?
  Full labour dispute cycle + no visa ? cannot afford to wait for adjudication ? abandon remedies
       ?                                    ?
  Low wages ? high remittances ? no savings ? cannot stop sending money ?
```

Each link reinforces the others:
- **Visa?employer bonding** deprives FDHs of the most basic freedom in any labour market ? the ability to choose between different employers
- **Inadequate wages** (MAW below the living wage) render FDHs unable to bear the time cost of seeking remedies
- **Mandatory live-in + working hours regulation exemption** deprives FDHs of the physical boundary that separates "off duty" from employment labour and personal life
- **The 14-day window + the protracted full dispute resolution cycle** transforms procedure itself into a mechanism of exclusion ? the question is not whether the procedure itself is fair, but that the fairness of the procedure presupposes that participants have the capacity to pay the time cost of the procedure

### 4.2 The Institutional Logic of "Contract Is Everything"

The legal status of FDHs in Hong Kong is almost entirely dependent on the Standard Employment Contract (ID 407). Once the contract is terminated, **all rights** ? lawful residence, work, medical care, etc. ? **cease immediately**. This is fundamentally different from the situation of local workers ? when a local worker loses employment, she remains a Hong Kong permanent resident, and basic rights such as medical care and residence do not cease upon termination of the employment relationship.

Every basic right of an FDH hangs on a legal thread that can be unilaterally severed by the employer. This institutional design of "contract as status" transforms the formal freedom of equal contracting parties into a substantively asymmetrical relationship of domination.

### 4.3 The Structural Role of Source Country Governments

The predicaments faced by FDHs cannot be attributed solely to Hong Kong's institutional framework and the behaviour of individual employers. The governments of source countries (the Philippines, Indonesia, etc.) play an equally critical role:

- Source country governments have failed to provide adequate employment, social security, and market conditions for their populations
- They establish **labour export policies**, shifting the responsibility of "sustaining the survival of their population" onto individual workers
- Workers go abroad to earn foreign exchange ? remittances flow back to the home country ? sustain basic family survival ? the government does not need to bear corresponding welfare expenditure
- The educational outcomes produced among workers' children (e.g., university graduates) feed back into the home country's human capital ? but the cost of education is borne by individual workers
- The government simultaneously plays the role of "protector" (consular protection, overseas labour offices), making its "collaborator" dimension less susceptible to simple confrontation

Workers are placed in the narrow space between **structural poverty in the source country** and **institutionalised low wages in the destination** ? this is not an individual "choice," but a structural position within the global division of labour.

### 4.4 Labour Stratification by Nationality and Ethnicity

The FDH population is predominantly of Philippine and Indonesian nationality, overwhelmingly female ? this **highly concentrated labour supply by nationality and gender** is structurally a continuation of the labour stratification arrangements of Hong Kong's colonial period. This stratification is not a spontaneous outcome of "market competition" but has been institutionalised as reality through the following mechanisms:

- "Foreign domestic helper" as a separate legal category, excluded from multiple general labour protections (Schedule 2 of the *Employment Ordinance*, exemption from the *Minimum Wage Ordinance*)
- The government maintains differential treatment through arguments of "cultural difference" and "the particular nature of live-in domestic labour"
- The politics of terminology ? the official positioning as "helper" rather than "worker" ? provides, at the institutional level, a legitimising discourse for treatment below local labour standards

### 4.5 The Intersection of Gender, Class, and the Global Care Chain

The labour of FDHs simultaneously possesses three characteristics:
- **Wage labour**: providing commodified domestic and care services to employers
- **Reproductive labour**: caring for the elderly and children ? this is a necessary component of total social labour but is typically not counted in national economic accounting
- **Emotional labour**: being expected to be "like family," expending emotional labour alongside physical and temporal labour

At the same time, the "Global Care Chain" reveals the transnational dimension of this phenomenon: a Filipina FDH cares for the elderly and children of an employer's family in Hong Kong ? her own children are cared for by a grandmother or relative in rural Philippines ? the grandmother's care needs are borne by even poorer relatives or community members. Transnational live-in labour is not an isolated phenomenon ? it is one link in the process by which contemporary global capitalism restructures social reproduction within the labour market.

## V. Directions for Local Advocacy

> This section objectively records the principal policy advocacy directions proposed by frontline actors and migrant worker groups, without making normative judgments.

1. **Adjust the Minimum Allowable Wage to the living wage level**: Raise the MAW from its current level to above HK$6,320/month, so that FDHs retain basic economic margin to sustain the process of seeking remedies when suffering rights violations
2. **Permit separation of residence**: Amend the mandatory live-in clause in the Standard Employment Contract to allow FDHs the option of not residing with their employer (living-out option), transforming the labour relationship from a "possession-of-the-person" model to an "employment" model
3. **Adjust administrative discretion ? grant work visas during disputes**: During the processing of labour disputes, the Immigration Department should approve work visas (rather than refuse by default) ? this is an adjustment within the scope of administrative discretion, not requiring legislative amendment
4. **Establish a dedicated adjudication channel for FDH labour disputes**: Shorten the full processing cycle for FDH cases to a bearable timeframe (frontline groups recommend a target of 3 months)
5. **Abolish the MAW/SMW dual-track system**: Bring FDHs within the scope of the statutory minimum wage under the *Minimum Wage Ordinance*
